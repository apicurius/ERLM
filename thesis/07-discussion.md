# 7. Discussion

This chapter interprets the experimental design of Chapters 5 and 6 in light of the falsifiable predictions stated in Chapter 1 and formalized in Chapter 3. Because the A/B pilot (control $\lambda=0$ vs. treatment $\lambda=0.2$) is in flight and has produced no completed result artifacts, the discussion is necessarily *conditional*: it lays out what the still-pending numbers would have to look like to confirm or falsify the central claim, then steps back to examine the limitations, the reward-hacking surface, the threats to validity, and the broader significance of optimizing *how* a Recursive Language Model (RLM) spends its budget rather than only *whether* it answers correctly.

> **[RESULTS PENDING]** No completed run artifacts exist at the time of writing. Every statement below about empirical outcomes is framed as a hypothesis or a decision rule, never as a finding. The only concrete numbers in this chapter are config constants from Chapter 5 and the labelled illustrative reward arithmetic of Section 3.5.

## 7.1 Interpreting the pending outcomes against P1–P4

The thesis stands or falls on a single, falsifiable proposition: that scaffold efficiency is a *separable, learnable* objective that can be added on top of a correctness-only RLM policy without trading away correctness. The four predictions P1–P4 from Section 5.1 operationalize that proposition, and the discussion is most useful if we state, in advance, exactly which patterns in the `analyze_pilot.py` output would count as confirmation and which would count as falsification.

**P1 — no correctness regression.** The treatment must satisfy $\text{acc}_{\text{treat}} \ge \text{acc}_{\text{ctrl}} - \epsilon$ on the *unshaped* held-out suite (OOLONG `trec_coarse`@131072 $n{=}50$, OOLONG-Pairs@32768 $n{=}20$, BrowseComp-Plus $k{=}50$ $n{=}150$, LongBench-v2 CodeQA $n{=}50$). P1 is the *guard* prediction: it is the one the entire reward design is engineered to make trivially true. Because the efficiency bonus is gated by `correct_threshold=1.0` and applied only to rollouts that already clear the correctness bar (Chapter 3, Dominance invariant), the gradient the treatment receives differs from the control's *only within the fully-correct set of rollouts for a given prompt*. The shaping term is structurally incapable of up-weighting a wrong-but-cheap trajectory. P1 would therefore be confirmed by an accuracy delta indistinguishable from zero (within the noise floor of $n{=}20$/env in the pilot, or $n{=}50$/$150$ at full scale). P1 would be *falsified* if the treatment's accuracy fell materially below the control's — and crucially, were that to happen, it would not falsify the *reward design* but would instead implicate one of two failure modes: (i) optimization interference, where pushing on cheap-but-correct trajectories perturbs the policy on the GRPO advantage signal in a way that bleeds into correctness, or (ii) verifier leakage, where the "correctness" the treatment optimized was a scorer loophole rather than genuine task success (Section 7.3).

**P2 — cheaper trajectories at equal accuracy.** This is the *headline* prediction. The treatment should use strictly fewer mean turns and/or sub-LLM calls and/or sub-LLM tokens at matched accuracy — a Pareto improvement on the accuracy-vs-cost plane. In `analyze_pilot.py`, this surfaces as the `## TRAJECTORY COST` block, where `COST_PAT` filters metrics matching `iter`/`sub_llm`/`subcall`/`token` and reports a percent delta $D\% = (\text{treat}_{\text{last}} - \text{ctrl}_{\text{last}})/\text{ctrl}_{\text{last}}\times 100$. Confirmation is a negative $D\%$ on one or more cost axes *while P1 holds*. The illustrative arithmetic of Section 3.5 makes concrete what the reward is rewarding: at the canonical budgets ($\lambda=0.2$; $B_{\text{turns}}=20$, $B_{\text{calls}}=32$, $B_{\text{tok}}=200000$; equal weights; $c=1$), a lean correct rollout (3 turns, 3 sub-calls, 35{,}000 tokens) earns $R \approx 1.1721$, whereas a wasteful correct rollout (18 turns, 30 sub-calls, 160{,}000 tokens) earns only $R \approx 1.0242$. Both tie at exactly $1.0$ under the correctness-only control. That reward gap ($\approx 0.148$) is the *entire* learning signal P2 depends on: if GRPO can convert it into a measurable shift in mean cost without disturbing correctness, P2 is confirmed.

**P3 — lower tail cost.** RLM cost is long-tailed and high-variance: the paper documents (\S F.2) tail trajectories that re-verify and re-generate many times [Zhang et al., 2026]. P3 predicts the treatment shrinks the $p95$ of turns/tokens. This is a stronger and arguably more important claim than P2, because the practical pain of RLM inference is the tail, not the mean. The monotonicity invariant (Chapter 3: holding $c$ fixed and correct, $R$ is non-increasing in each budget-axis usage) means the steepest reward penalty is applied precisely to the most wasteful correct trajectories — exactly the tail P3 targets. The pilot's $n{=}20$/env is almost certainly too small to estimate a $p95$ reliably; P3 is properly a *full-scale* prediction ($n{=}50$/$150$, `max_steps=200`).

**P4 — generalization.** The efficiency gain should transfer to a held-out task family or larger context, mirroring the paper's length-generalization result (Observation 6: a post-trained RLM-Qwen3-8B reported $3.2\times$–$9.6\times$ faster with 68–90% less runtime) [Zhang et al., 2026]. In our suite, the natural held-out probe is LongBench-v2 CodeQA (a code-repository task not in the OOLONG-Spam/BrowseComp-Plus training mix) and the context-length jump from training contexts in $[32768, 65536]$ to the $131072$ eval. P4 is confirmed if the cost reduction observed on in-distribution evals persists on the held-out family; it is the prediction most likely to remain weakly powered in any single pilot, and the one whose confirmation would most strongly support the claim that the model has learned *orchestration discipline* rather than memorized a per-dataset shortcut.

**Joint falsification criterion.** The thesis as a whole is falsified if, *across reasonable $\lambda$* (the pre-registered sweep $\{0.05, 0.1, 0.2\}$), the treatment cannot beat the control on P2/P3 without violating P1. That is, if every Pareto-frontier point that buys a cost reduction also costs accuracy, then efficiency and correctness are *not* separable under this objective and base model, and the central claim fails. Confirmation requires at least one frontier point where cost drops and accuracy is preserved.

## 7.2 Limitations

**Single base model and compute budget.** All conclusions are conditioned on one base model, `Qwen/Qwen3-30B-A3B-Instruct-2507`, a 30B-A3B MoE instruct model used in non-thinking mode (`enable_thinking=false`), adapted with a small LoRA (rank 32, $\alpha=64$, targeting `q_proj`/`k_proj`/`v_proj`/`o_proj`) [Hu et al., 2021; Qwen Team, 2025]. The claim that efficiency is learnable *at fixed model and compute* (Chapter 3, third rejected claim) is what gives the experiment its scientific cleanliness, but it is also the limitation: we cannot say whether the effect scales, attenuates, or reverses on a different family, a thinking-mode model, or a full fine-tune. A negative pilot result would not generalize to "efficiency is unlearnable"; a positive one would not generalize to "efficiency shaping always helps."

**Pilot scale.** The in-flight feasibility study (configs `pilot-l0.toml` = control, `pilot-l02.toml` = treatment) is deliberately small: `max_steps=10`, `group_size=2`, `gpu_memory_utilization=0.90`, `enforce_eager=true`, inference `tp=4/dp=1`, and eval $n{=}20$/env, A40-tuned and launched via `scripts/valar_pilot.sh`. At ten optimization steps and twenty evaluation examples per environment, the pilot can establish *feasibility and direction* — does cost move, does accuracy hold, do the telemetry channels light up — but it cannot establish *significance*. Tail statistics (P3) and generalization (P4) require the full-scale configs (`rlm-qwen3-30b-thesis.toml` / `-control.toml`, `max_steps=200`, eval $n{=}50/20/150/50$). The pilot's role is to de-risk the full run, not to substitute for it; reading a P2 trend off ten steps is suggestive, not conclusive.

**Token-telemetry coverage gaps.** The token efficiency axis (`rlm_sub_llm_tokens`, $B_{\text{tok}}=200000$) is only as live as the usage metadata flowing back from the sub-LLM proxy. `env.py`'s `_record_sub_call` accumulates `total_tokens` (or `prompt_tokens + completion_tokens`) from each sub-call's usage dict, but when usage is absent or malformed it instead increments `rlm_sub_llm_usage_missing` rather than fabricating a count. If that counter is large, the token axis silently under-counts cost: a rollout can spend tokens that never enter $e$, making the token bonus a partial no-op and biasing the treatment toward whichever cost axis happens to be fully instrumented (turns and sub-calls are counted directly and are not subject to this gap). This is why `analyze_pilot.py`'s decision-rule read explicitly flags `rlm_sub_llm_usage_missing ~ 0` as a precondition ("else token efficiency is a no-op"). Any interpretation of a *token*-axis result must first confirm the missing-usage counter is near zero; a token-cost reduction reported against a high missing-usage baseline is uninterpretable.

**Judge dependence for BrowseComp-Plus.** Two of the four eval environments (BrowseComp-Plus, in both train and eval) score via an LLM judge (`reward_mode="judge"`, `judge_model="openai/gpt-4.1"`) that returns a JSON `is_correct` boolean [BrowseComp-Plus]. The correctness signal $c$ on this task is therefore only as faithful as the judge. This introduces three coupled risks: judge non-determinism (the same trajectory may be scored differently across calls), judge cost and latency (each scored rollout is an extra API call, bounded by the `timeout_seconds=1200` per-rollout guard), and judge *availability* — if the judge call fails, the deterministic containment fallback `_score_browsecomp_plus` returns $1.0$ on mutual substring containment (`env.py:197-210`), a far weaker criterion. Because the efficiency bonus is gated on $c$, a flaky judge that intermittently falls back to containment quietly weakens the gate, and the consequences of that are the subject of the next section.

## 7.3 Reward hacking, in depth

Adding an efficiency term does not create new reward-hacking opportunities out of nothing; it *amplifies every pre-existing weakness in the correctness signal $c$* [Skalse et al., 2022; Amodei et al., 2016]. The intuition is Goodhart's law applied compositionally: the policy is now rewarded for the *cheapest* trajectory the scorer still calls correct, so any gap between "scored correct" and "actually correct" becomes a gradient pointing straight at the loophole. This section examines why the gating is necessary but not sufficient, and what hardening the design therefore requires.

**Gating is necessary but not sufficient.** The correctness gate (`correct_threshold=1.0`, applied via `_base_value` / `_passes_gates` before any bonus) guarantees the Dominance invariant: a wrong-but-cheap rollout can never outscore a right-but-expensive one, and $R \in [0,\, c(1+\lambda)]$ with the bonus capped at $\lambda$. This rules out the *crudest* hack — the one the rejected baseline $R = c - \mu\cdot\text{cost}$ admits, where a premature `FINAL` or a plan-masquerading-as-answer can make wrong-but-cheap beat right-but-expensive (Chapter 3, first rejected claim). But gating is *necessary*, not *sufficient*, because it assumes $c$ is faithful. The gate is a function of the scorer's verdict, so if the scorer can be fooled cheaply, the gate fires on a false positive and the efficiency bonus *rewards the fooling*. Gating moves the attack surface from "beat correctness with cost" to "satisfy the scorer's letter while violating its intent, as cheaply as possible."

**Verifier hardening.** Two concrete loopholes in the present scorers illustrate the surface. First, the OOLONG-synth substring fallback: `_oolong_synth_score` returns $1.0$ when `gold_s.lower()` is a substring of the output (`env.py:146-150`, excluding the comparison phrases themselves). A policy could learn to emit a short string containing the gold token without genuinely inspecting the 32k–131k-token context — and the efficiency reward would *prefer* exactly such a context-skipping shortcut because it is cheap. Second, the BrowseComp-Plus containment fallback: `_score_browsecomp_plus` returns $1.0$ on mutual containment when `reward_mode != "judge"` or the judge call fails (`env.py:197-210`). The mitigations, stated as policy in Chapter 5, are: (i) gate on the *strictest available* $c$ — for BrowseComp-Plus, the LLM judge, never the containment proxy; (ii) tighten or disable the lenient fallbacks specifically for the shaped arm; (iii) optionally gate on a *second independent* correctness check; and (iv) require evidence of *genuine work* — not merely `min_iterations>=2`, but evidence the context was actually inspected, since `min_iterations` counts turns, not substantive engagement with the data.

**Canaries.** Static loophole-closing is reactive; canaries are proactive. The anti-reward-hacking protocol (Chapter 5) prescribes adversarial canaries: prompts engineered so that the *obvious short answer is wrong unless the context is genuinely inspected*. A policy that has learned a context-skipping shortcut will fail canaries while scoring well on ordinary items — a discriminative signal that scalar reward alone cannot provide. Canaries operationalize the principle that we must audit *trajectories*, not just rewards (protocol point 5): two policies with identical scalar reward distributions can differ entirely in whether they read the context.

**Why cost-down *plus* fallback-up equals hacking.** The single most important diagnostic in this chapter is a *joint* one. A drop in trajectory cost (P2/P3 looking good) is *not* progress if it coincides with a rising rate of fallback-hit or lenient-scorer activation. The two signatures together — cheaper trajectories *and* more reliance on the substring/containment fallbacks (or a rising `rlm_sub_llm_usage_missing`, which can hide the true token cost) — are the canonical signature of the efficiency reward eating a scorer loophole rather than teaching orchestration discipline. This is why `analyze_pilot.py` reports `## SHORTCUT / HEALTH DIAGNOSTICS` (`DIAG_PAT` over `usage_missing`/`efficiency`/`shaped`/`gibberish`/`repetition`/`truncat`/`zero_advantage`) *alongside* the cost block: the decision rule is explicitly "NOT shortcutting" $\equiv$ treatment accuracy $\gtrsim$ control AND the shortcut diagnostics are flat. Any cost reduction that coincides with a rising fallback-hit rate must be treated as reward hacking, not as a result.

## 7.4 Threats to validity and mitigations

The validity case rests on the *faithful-twin* construction (Chapter 5), and it is worth recapping the threats it neutralizes and the residual ones it does not.

| Threat | Mitigation |
|---|---|
| Confounded A/B (treatment differs from control in more than the reward) | `tools/validate_thesis_shaping.py` flattens both TOMLs and asserts the diff set is a subset of the allowed keys (`output_dir`, `wandb.name`, two LoRA adapter names, two train-env names, two `shaping_coef` values); everything else — model, LoRA, optimizer, caps, sampling, filters, training split, eval suite — is byte-identical |
| $\lambda=0$ silently differs from upstream $R=c$ | Parity invariant: at `shaping_coef==0.0` the shaped reward is byte-identical to the stock gated reward; pinned by `test_parity_with_stock_rubric_when_coef_zero` |
| Optimizing the publication metric directly (training-on-the-test-objective) | Eval suite is *unshaped* (no `shaping_coef` on any eval env); train reward and headline metric (the accuracy-vs-cost frontier) are separated (protocol point 7) |
| Wrong control choice | The validator forbids `rlm-qwen3-30b-efficient-eval-suite.toml` as control because its single-env mix changes the *training distribution*; the faithful twin preserves the 0.5/0.5 OOLONG-Spam / BrowseComp-Plus split |
| Per-prompt rollout variance swamps the effect | Paired A/B with identical prompts and GRPO grouping (`group_size=8` full, `2` pilot); per-group advantage $s_i - \text{mean}(s)$ controls within-prompt baseline [Shao et al., 2024] |
| Caps already do the efficiency work (redundancy) | Caps are *ceilings*, not preferences: two correct cap-satisfying rollouts can differ greatly in cost yet tie at $R=c$; the reward credit-assigns *within* that set (pinned by `test_structural_caps_leave_correctness_only_rollouts_tied`) |
| Reward measures a scorer loophole, not the task | Section 7.3 hardening: strictest-available $c$, disabled fallbacks for the shaped arm, canaries, trajectory audits |

The residual, *unmitigated* threats are the ones flagged in Section 7.2: single base model, pilot statistical power, token-telemetry gaps, and judge dependence. None of these is fixed by the twin construction; they bound the *strength* of any conclusion, not its *internal* validity.

## 7.5 Broader impact and ethics

The practical motivation is straightforward: long-context RLM inference is expensive and its cost is long-tailed [Zhang et al., 2026], so an objective that shrinks mean and especially tail cost makes near-infinite-context reasoning cheaper and more predictable to deploy. The corresponding risk is the mirror image of the benefit. An efficiency objective, however carefully gated, applies optimization pressure toward *cheap* trajectories, and "cheap" is not always "good": a policy can learn to produce trajectories that are shallow-but-cheap and that happen to pass an imperfect scorer, especially on tasks where a lucky short answer is occasionally right. Left unaudited, such a system would systematically under-inspect its context — precisely the behavior the canary tests are designed to expose.

The safeguards that make the *research* sound are also the ethical safeguards. Keeping evaluation *unshaped* means the reported metric cannot be gamed by the training objective. Auditing trajectories rather than scalars, stress-testing with adversarial canaries, sweeping $\lambda$ and keeping only Pareto-frontier points, and treating any cost-down/fallback-up co-movement as hacking rather than progress — together these ensure that a reported efficiency gain reflects genuine orchestration discipline and not a quietly-shallower policy. The honest framing of the headline as an *accuracy-vs-cost frontier plot*, not a single cost number, keeps the correctness cost of any efficiency claim visible. We also note the benchmark-hygiene obligation: BrowseComp-Plus ships canary-encrypted gold answers under a "BENCHMARK DATA SHOULD NEVER APPEAR online" marker, and any release of trajectories or logs must preserve that canary discipline to avoid contaminating future training corpora.

## 7.6 Relation to prior efficiency and process-reward work

This work sits at the intersection of three lines. From **RL-with-verifiable-rewards** [Lambert et al., 2024; Ouyang et al., 2022] it inherits the correctness signal $c$ and the discipline of grounding reward in a checkable verdict; its contribution is to ask what to do *after* correctness is verified, when many correct trajectories tie. From **potential-based reward shaping** [Ng et al., 1999] it borrows the intuition that auxiliary reward terms must not change the set of optimal policies in a way that corrupts the primary objective — here realized not as a potential function but as strict *subordination*: efficiency re-ranks only within the fully-correct set and is a no-op ($e=0$) when no axis is enabled, so the optimal-correctness policy set is preserved by construction (Chapter 3). From the **process-reward and agent-efficiency** literature it shares the goal of shaping *how* a model acts, not only its final answer; the distinguishing commitment here is that the signal, not merely the instruction, must carry the gradient — prompting alone (the second rejected claim) gives the model no credit-assignment distinguishing a lean correct trajectory from a wasteful one. The RLM/CodeAct lineage [Zhang et al., 2026; Wang et al., 2024] supplies the substrate — context-as-REPL-variable, sub-LLM-calls-as-functions — within which "efficiency" has a concrete, measurable meaning across the turns, sub-call, and token axes. Where the foundational RLM work derived efficiency *implicitly* from structural levers (orchestrator addenda forbidding tiny-prompt mega-batches, non-thinking sampling, `max_iterations=20`, exec timeouts) and *never an explicit objective* [Zhang et al., 2026], this thesis's wager is that making it an explicit, correctness-gated objective — $R = c\,(1 + \lambda e)$ — turns an emergent side-effect into a directly optimizable, auditable target. Whether that wager pays off is, at the time of writing, the open empirical question Chapter 6 is reserved to answer.
